Five directions · v1

Five ways to look exactly right.

The current mark is a gradient rounded-square "G". It is competent, but it reads as a Canva lookalike: the same tile, the same blue-to-cyan gradient, the same soft corners that a hundred other apps already wear. Low ownability is the real cost. Below are five distinct identity directions, each with a logo mark, a full color palette, and a type pairing you can actually license. Every one is built from one idea the name hands us for free.

Where we are

G
Gradient rounded-square G.
Indigo → cyan, soft corners.

The tile-plus-gradient-letter format is the most copied logo shape of the last decade. It signals "an app" but says nothing about German, precision, or the plateau these learners are trying to break. Every direction below deliberately steps away from the gradient tile.

The idea the name gives us

Genau.

Genauly is built on genau, the German word for "exactly / precisely", and the affirmation you say out loud the moment something clicks: "Genau!" So the brand's spine is precision and the satisfying snap of getting it right. Each direction below is a different way to draw that.

Genau.

the confident full stop

No tile at all. The mark is the letterform plus a single red period, the punctuation of certainty. It reads as a considered editorial brand, closer to a publisher than an app, which suits adult learners and exam candidates who want to be taken seriously. The dot is the whole system: it ends the wordmark, dots the favicon, and becomes the "correct" tick inside the product.

Wasserwaage

the bubble sits dead-center — perfectly level, genau

A spirit-level vial with the bubble resting exactly between the two etched lines. "Level" and "exact" are the same gesture, and it is a real object, so it is instantly legible at 16px. The language is Braun / Dieter Rams: matte, flat, one safety-orange accent doing all the talking. No gradient anywhere, which is the sharpest possible break from the current tile.

Umlaut

the two dots — the most German mark there is

German owns a symbol almost no other brand uses: the umlaut, two dots. Here they float over the G and hover above the wordmark, doubling as a progress pair inside the product (one filled = halfway, two filled = mastered). It is playful and smart without being childish, and it plants a flag: this app is about German specifically, not "languages" in general.

Zielband — hitting your level

mark / warm · target device
favicon · on dark

Zielband

the target, with a marker pinned at B2

A target with a bullseye and a small marker notch at the top: your level, hit exactly. This is the warmest, most motivating direction. Where the plateau story is "you are stuck at B1", the target says "here is the ring you are aiming for, and you can land in it". The concentric rings map naturally onto CEFR bands (A2 → B1 → B2 → C1) as a progress motif.

Neuland

the summit above the plateau line

A contour peak rising off a dashed plateau line, the exact story of the product: B1 to B2 is a flat stretch, and this is the climb out with the summit in gold. It is the only direction that ties directly to the in-app Neuland game world, so brand and game speak one visual language. Earthy, warm, and outdoorsy without a single cliché mountain-outline startup vibe, because the plateau line is the twist.

How I'd choose

If you want the app to feel credible and grown-up for exam prep, go Genau. or Wasserwaage. If you want it to feel like a warm consumer product with a strong game hook, go Zielband or Neuland. Umlaut is the boldest middle path and the most ownable "this is a German app" statement. My single pick: Neuland, because it is the only one that unifies the brand with the game you are already building.
